How I work

Background

My design approach

Where I'm starting from

I came to UI/UX design through software engineering rather than a traditional design background. As a first-year student in the BASc Software Engineering program at the University of Ottawa, I am currently taking SEG3125 — Analysis and Design of User Interfaces — as my formal introduction to design principles. Outside of the classroom, I have built personal projects ranging from a drone navigation algorithm to various web applications, which gave me an appreciation for how much the quality of an interface affects the experience of using a product. That gap between functional and well-designed is what draws me to UX.

What I'm learning

Through SEG3125 and the Nielsen Norman Group resources used in the course, I am currently exploring the foundational principles of visual communication — including scale, visual hierarchy, balance, and contrast — and how these principles translate into interface decisions. Later in the semester I will be building on these fundamentals with user-centered design methodologies, heuristic evaluation, and interaction design patterns. This portfolio will grow alongside that learning.

Process

My workflow

01

Understand

Before touching any tool, I ask myself three questions: What problem am I solving? Who am I designing for? What are the key user needs? From there I ask what I already know and what I am missing — filling those gaps in knowledge before committing to any direction. This prevents building on assumptions that will create more work later.

02

Explore

I always begin exploration by hand — sketching layouts, testing colour palettes, and trying different typographic approaches on paper before moving to any digital tool. This keeps the ideation phase fast and low-commitment. I look at reference sites both as inspiration and as examples of what to avoid, then narrow down to a direction that fits the problem and the audience.

03

Design & refine

Once a direction is set, I move into code to build a more polished and interactive version of the design. I treat the first coded version as a draft — something to test, critique, and iterate on rather than a final product. Refinement is where most of the real design decisions happen, as seeing something rendered at full scale reveals issues that sketches never will.
